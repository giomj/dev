# Grand Council — Session 3 — Historian-Philosopher Opening

*Seat: Historian-Philosopher. Convened 2026-07-18. On artifacts J (n2yo), K (SatDump), L (GOGPT), M (integration plan).*

## J — n2yo.com

Real-time satellite tracking descends from a Cold War act of state cartography: the U.S. Space Surveillance Network, born in the scramble after Sputnik 1's launch on 4 October 1957 (rocket body cataloged as object 1, the satellite itself as object 2), whose master catalog of every human-made object in orbit is the ancestor of every tracker since. That catalog was maintained by NORAD and, from 1985, by USSPACECOM (merged into USSTRATCOM in 2002, USSPACECOM re-established 2019, the register now run by the 18th Space Defense Squadron), and it remains the single source of record — the two-line element sets and SGP4 propagation that n2yo serves are not n2yo's invention but the state's format. The decisive civic turn came in 1985, when Dr. T.S. Kelso — whose initials, fittingly, stand for "Tracking Satellites" — began distributing TLEs first from a Sunnyvale bulletin board and then via CelesTrak from Austin, transforming a classified-adjacent military product into a free public utility for operators, educators, and amateurs. n2yo belongs to the third generation of that lineage: the browser-native amateur online trackers of the 2000s that wrapped the NORAD catalog in maps, footprints, pass predictions, and SMS/voice ISS alerts for a general audience. Its genre is therefore the *operational skin*, not the source — a real-time convenience layer (34,799 objects, a proprietary 57-category taxonomy, rate-limited REST) over data it neither generates nor authenticates. The public good it delivers is best understood as **derived legibility**: it makes an existing, state-held commons *usable in the moment* — the difference between owning the census and being able to see who is above your rooftop tonight. Its epistemic humility is the correct posture; it is a courier of the record, and the council should cite it as an aggregator, never as ground truth.

## K — SatDump

Amateur satellite reception is one of the oldest citizen-science practices of the space age, reaching back to the Moonwatch and Project Moonbeam volunteers who tracked Sputnik in 1957 and, from the 1970s onward, to hobbyists building APT and HRPT ground stations to pull weather imagery directly from NOAA POES and Meteor spacecraft. For a generation the tooling was scarce and often closed: WxToImg was the canonical APT decoder of the 2000s but went unmaintained/defunct, leaving a community dependent on abandonware — the classic fragility of a craft whose tools outlive their maintainers. The rupture came in early 2012, when Antti Palosaari discovered that cheap Realtek RTL2832U DVB-T television dongles could be repurposed as wideband software-defined radios, and outlets like Hackaday and the rtl-sdr.com community turned a ~$20 receiver into a mass instrument — the democratization event that made a ground station affordable to anyone. SatDump is the mature artifact of that post-2012 open-source culture: a GPL-family, community-maintained suite that abstracts the whole receive chain — demodulate, deframe, decode, compose, geo-reference — into declarative *pipelines*, so that supporting a new satellite becomes configuration rather than new code, an architectural move in the lineage of GNU Radio flowgraphs but lifted to the satellite-processing semantic layer. As artifact it represents the consolidation phase of an open-source movement: not the first tool but the one that outgrew and replaced the closed incumbents (WxToImg, Meteor Demodulator), spanning six mainstream SDRs plus the AD9361/Pluto family across desktop, Raspberry Pi, Docker, and F-Droid Android. Philosophically it embodies the hacker-commons wager that infrastructure once reserved to meteorological agencies can be reconstituted, auditably and for free, on a hobbyist's desk — the "ground-station-in-a-box" as a monument to reproducible, forkable public knowledge.

## L — Global Oil & Gas Plant Tracker (GOGPT)

Plant-level energy inventory has, for over a century, been the domain of proprietary commercial intelligence: the UDI/Platts World Electric Power Plants Database traces its lineage to 1911, sold as a paid product to utilities, financiers, and governments — authoritative, but enclosed behind a paywall and answerable to its customers. A partial opening came in 2018 with the World Resources Institute's Global Power Plant Database, the first genuinely open, research-grade global inventory (~29,000 plants) — an authoritative nonprofit dataset, but a broad multi-fuel snapshot rather than a continuously curated, campaign-oriented tracker. Global Energy Monitor's GOGPT occupies a distinct and politically consequential niche: an open-license (CC BY 4.0), bi-annually updated, methodology-footnoted, peer-reviewed independent aggregator that sums oil- and gas-fired capacity from the bottom up (1,047 GW in development as of the January 2026 release) and, crucially, validates its rows *against* both Platts and the WRI database while adding per-plant wiki pages, status transitions, and CO₂ accounting. Its political-epistemic role is that of a **counter-archive**: it takes the plant as the unit of accountability, attaches sources and public-opposition context to each, and licenses the whole so that no permission bottleneck stands between the public and the fossil-fuel buildout. This is why its citation by the IEA and IPCC matters beyond convenience — an intergovernmental body that grounds its numbers in a transparent, CC-licensed, footnoted tracker inherits an auditable evidentiary chain rather than a black-boxed commercial estimate, shifting authority from "trust the vendor" to "check the footnote." The obligation such an aggregator assumes is honesty about tier and provenance — GOGPT's own methodology flags location accuracy and, as the council's C3 notes, that the U.S. IRP-supplement is *planning intent*, not committed construction. It is the emblem of a wider movement: independent, open, methodologically legible aggregation as a form of civic infrastructure — the commonplace-book of the energy transition, kept in public.

## M — Integration plan

`conceptual-engineering-references` is best read not as a codebase but as a **curated reference shelf** — a deliberate act of collection whose intellectual work is selection, tiering, and cross-linking rather than invention. It joins three overlapping traditions at once. First, the **engineering handbook** in the lineage of *Marks' Standard Handbook for Mechanical Engineers* (first edition 1916) and the *CRC Handbook* — the ambition to gather trustworthy, load-bearing references so a practitioner can build on settled ground; the plan's evidence-tier gradient (GOGPT peer-reviewed > SatDump operational reference > n2yo aggregator) is exactly the handbook's discipline of telling the reader how much weight each source can bear. Second, the **commonplace book** — the Renaissance-through-Enlightenment practice of transcribing passages into a personal register organized for reuse — visible here in the `notes/` and `samples/` folders and in the bridging links to sibling projects (gravitational-compass, grid-and-chain-mobile, recursive-state-dynamics), which are annotations in the commonplacer's spirit of "where does this belong, and to what does it connect." Third, the **canonical-reference-shelf** or personal canon: the conscious decision that *these* artifacts, and not others, deserve a permanent seat. The tradition it joins carries binding obligations that the plan already anticipates: fidelity to each source's license (respecting the strictest binding — n2yo's ToS and rate-limited key — while honoring GOGPT's CC BY 4.0 attribution strings and SatDump's GPL); honesty about tier, so no aggregator is quoted as if it were primary; and honesty about cadence, since a real-time skin, a quarterly-ish software release, and a bi-annual dataset each freeze truth at a different rate and must never be silently cross-dated. A reference shelf, unlike a mere download folder, promises its future reader that every item has been vetted, situated, and correctly labeled — that is the curator's duty this project takes on.
