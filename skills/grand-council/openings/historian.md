### Artifact A

On C6, the "Father of Chaos Theory" epithet is defensible but should be historicized: Edward Lorenz's 1963 paper "Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow" (Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences) crystallized C1 and C3, yet it stands on real precursors. Henri Poincaré's 1890 work on the three-body problem already exhibited sensitive dependence, and Stephen Smale's horseshoe map (early 1960s) supplied the topological machinery for chaos. Lorenz is better named a founder than the sole father. C5 requires correction: the "butterfly" framing entered the record through the title of Lorenz's 1972 AAAS talk, "Does the Flap of a Butterfly's Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?"—but that title was proposed by meteorologist Philip Merilees, not coined by Lorenz. The earlier metaphor was a seagull. The naming is thus collaborative folklore, not authorial decree.

### Artifact B

C1 and C2 stand on settled ground, so the historian's note is brief. This pedagogy sits atop a genuine lineage: Arthur Cayley's 1858 "A Memoir on the Theory of Matrices" first treated matrices as objects with their own algebra, giving addition, multiplication, and the identity their formal footing—the very operations Page B1 restates. The Hermitian and skew-Hermitian classes (Page B3) descend from Charles Hermite's mid-nineteenth-century work on forms with real spectra, later foundational to quantum mechanics. Nothing here is contested or repackaged; it is faithful, unobjectionable reference material transmitting a canon roughly 170 years old. The only meaning-question worth flagging is provenance hygiene: an Instagram-adjacent handwritten card carries no attribution to Cayley or Hermite, so students inherit the results detached from the names and problems that produced them.

### Artifact C

C5 is the crux, and it fails: PKM-BLT is self-published, uncited cosmology, not a framework that supersedes ΛCDM. Historically it belongs to a recognizable genre—the social-media "Theory of Everything" one-pager—following a fixed formula: yellow background, a "master equation," a "locked" fundamental constant, and an identity (C1's H₀·tᵤ = 1) that is a definitional tautology, since tᵤ ≡ 1/H₀ by construction. The Hubble radius (C6) and H₀·tᵤ ≈ 1 are standard textbook relations, presented here as if original. The eleven-significant-figure H₀ (C7) signals false precision when measurements disagree at the third figure. Compared with Stephen Wolfram's Physics Project, Eric Weinstein's Geometric Unity, or Nassim Haramein's work—all self-published at varying rigor—PKM-BLT sits at the low-rigor end: its "leak" mechanism is undefined and "cosmic controller" is not physics vocabulary. This is repackaged mysticism, not lineage.

### Artifact D

C1 correctly identifies real intellectual lineage: the topology descends from Vladimiro Mazzilli's ZVS driver, circulated on hobbyist forums in the mid-2000s and originally intended for flyback transformer and Tesla-coil driving before its migration to induction heating. That heritage is authentic and well documented in the maker community. The image itself, however, is a different artifact: the "1000W High-Power Induction Heater" render matches a common style of AI-generated or vector-illustrated Chinese kit marketing imagery, where visual polish outruns engineering fidelity. This framing matters for C3: the asymmetric 12 V / 22 V Zener clamp is almost certainly an illustration error—canonical Mazzilli builds use matched gate-clamp Zeners (typically ~12–18 V, symmetric)—rather than a deliberate design choice. Read the schematic as decorative provenance, not a buildable specification; the lineage is genuine while this particular depiction is unreliable.

### Artifact E

C1–C3 rest on deep lineage: the continuity principle descends from Leonhard Euler's 1757 equations of fluid motion, later formalized within Augustin-Louis Cauchy's early-nineteenth-century continuum mechanics, so Ganjir's Q = A·v and A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ transmit a canon roughly 270 years old. The hydraulic-analogy pedagogy is equally traditional—Kirchhoff's circuit laws have been taught via water-pipe metaphors since the nineteenth century—so the garden-hose framing is legitimate teaching craft. C4 is where scrutiny lands. The highway-traffic analogy is not merely loose; it has its own genuine lineage in the Lighthill–Whitham (1955) and Richards (1956) LWR model, which crucially makes velocity density-dependent and admits shockwaves—phenomena incompressible continuity cannot produce. The analogy is pedagogically useful for conveying conservation, but technically inexact: real traffic is a compressible, nonlinear flow, and presenting it as clean continuity conflates two distinct theories that history kept separate.
