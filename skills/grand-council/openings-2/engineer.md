## Artifact F

As a link-budget reference this poster is unusable, and would mislead a junior engineer if treated as design input. C6/C7 list bands and applications but the poster gives no EIRP, G/T, path loss, or rain-fade margin — you cannot size a link without free-space loss (~148 dB @ Ku, 12 GHz, GEO slant range) plus rain attenuation, which is severe at Ku and dominant at Ka (C6) yet unmentioned. C7's mapping of L-band to "mobile sat navigation" conflates GNSS ranging (one-way, no return-link transponder) with the "receive-amplify-retransmit" transponder model in C1 — GNSS satellites don't relay user traffic. The poster also omits latency, the single most consequential system-design parameter: GEO (C4, 35,786 km) imposes ~240 ms one-way propagation delay, unworkable for interactive control loops, while LEO's "low latency" claim (C5) is directionally correct (~1-10 ms) but omits inter-satellite handoff and Doppler compensation costs. Fine as an orientation graphic; do not design against it.

## Artifact G

For buildability, C1 (Bernoulli) is the equation students misuse in practice by dropping the loss term — real pipe systems need the extended form with h_L (friction + fitting losses); using ideal Bernoulli sizes a pump wrong every time. C5's Venturi meter is incomplete as a design reference: actual flow rate requires a discharge coefficient C_d (~0.98) applied to the theoretical velocity from the pressure drop — omitting it (as this slide does) overpredicts flow by 2%+, which compounds in cascaded calculations. C6's cavitation description is qualitatively correct but not actionable; the design rule engineers actually apply is NPSH_available > NPSH_required (with margin, typically ≥0.6 m), not just "pressure falls below vapor pressure." C2's Reynolds transition band (2000–4000) is reasonable for pipe flow. I abstain on C3/C4 — straightforward and not failure-mode-relevant.

## Artifact H

C1's claim that higher compression ratio r "generally improves efficiency" needs a caveat this slide omits: in SI engines r is capped by knock, which depends on fuel octane rating — pushing r without matching fuel invites detonation and engine damage, not efficiency gains. In CI engines the limit shifts to peak pressure, NOx, and soot rather than knock; the slide doesn't distinguish these failure modes (C2, C3). C3's Diesel efficiency formula correctly uses cutoff ratio ρ, but if ρ isn't defined on the slide itself, students will misapply the Otto formula (C2) to Diesel cycles. C5's BP = 2πNT/60 is dimensionally trustworthy only if T is torque in N·m and N in rpm, giving BP in watts — a common student error is plugging in T in kgf·m or N in rad/s without conversion. I abstain on the P-V diagram sign/area convention itself — that's better judged against the source figure directly than from the claim list.

## Artifact I

C3's 3.517 kW for 1 TR is fine — the exact value is 3.5168525 kW, so 3.517 is correct to 4 significant figures and adequate for engineering use. C2's RE = h1 − h4 is standard but the slide should state explicitly that h1 is evaporator exit (compressor inlet) and h4 is expansion-device exit (evaporator inlet); for the idealized VCRS, expansion is isenthalpic, so h4 = h3 (condenser exit) — if that equivalence isn't shown, students will search for a nonexistent separate h4 value on the p-h chart instead of just carrying h3 across the throttle. C1's COP definition is sound and matches how techs actually rate systems. I abstain on psychrometric chart layout correctness (C5, C6) — that's a graphical/thermodynamic-property judgment outside buildability/failure-mode charge; defer to Physicist.
