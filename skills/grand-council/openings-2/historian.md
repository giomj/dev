## Artifact F — Satellite Communication infographic

The whole poster rests on **C4**, the geostationary altitude of 35,786 km — and that figure is not a discovery but an *inheritance*. Arthur C. Clarke sketched it in his 1945 *Wireless World* piece "Extra-Terrestrial Relays," a full twelve years before Sputnik (1957) proved orbit at all, and nineteen before Syncom 3 (1964) made the GEO relay real. **C6**'s band labels (L, C, Ku, Ka) are the poster's tell: these are not a rational carving of the spectrum but WWII radar deception-code residue, a legacy artifact frozen into IEEE convention. The **C2/C3/C5** orbit tiers likewise encode a lived history — Iridium's 1998 LEO constellation and Starlink's 2020s revival. Pedagogically this is the *vendor-poster* genre: marketing dressed as taxonomy, ahistorical and uncited. It flattens fifty years of contingent engineering into tidy boxes.

## Artifact G — Fluid Mechanics slide 11 (Sanjiv Ganjir)

This slide is a synopsis of the eighteenth century's great settling of accounts. **C1**, Bernoulli's head equation, descends directly from Daniel Bernoulli's *Hydrodynamica* (1738), later given its differential rigor by Euler's equations (1757); **C4** carries Pascal's *Treatise on the Equilibrium of Liquids* (1653), and **C5**'s Venturi meter honors Giovanni Venturi (1797). The most historically honest claim is **C2**: Osborne Reynolds's 1883 dye-in-pipe experiment established the *number* but not a crisp threshold — the 2000/4000 transition band the slide gives has always been fuzzy, a range not a line. **C6**'s cavitation entered engineering only when 1890s ship propellers began pitting. Pedagogically this sits squarely in the hand-drawn "conceptual engineering" one-pager tradition, heir to *Marks' Standard Handbook* — synthesis over derivation, the master's distilled marginalia.

## Artifact H — IC Engines slide 9 (Sanjiv Ganjir)

Here the lineage is a genuine rivalry made pedagogy. **C2**, the Otto efficiency, memorializes Nikolaus Otto's 1876 four-stroke spark-ignition engine; **C3**'s Diesel cycle traces to Rudolf Diesel's 1893 patent and 1897 first working engine — two competing combustion philosophies (constant volume versus constant pressure) preserved side by side. Above both looms the ceiling neither slide names explicitly but every claim obeys: Sadi Carnot's 1824 *Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire*, which is why **C1**'s "higher r improves efficiency" is bounded, not open. The P–V diagram convention itself is Clapeyron's 1834 graphical gift. Genre matters: this is the twentieth-century engineering-school "cheat sheet," the conceptual one-pager descended from *Marks' Standard Handbook* — a tradition that trusts the diagram to carry what prose cannot.

## Artifact I — Refrigeration & Air Conditioning slide 10 (Sanjiv Ganjir)

This slide braids two histories: mechanical refrigeration and the science of moist air. **C4**'s vapor-compression cycle begins with Jacob Perkins's 1834 patent; **C3**'s "ton of refrigeration" is an Americanism from the 1880s ice trade — a commercial unit fossilized into thermodynamics, its 12,000 Btu/h a memorial to selling coldness by the block. The p–h plotting of **C4** owes to Mollier's 1904 enthalpy diagram. Most tellingly, **C5**'s psychrometry was not a discipline until the air-conditioning industry demanded it: Willis Carrier's 1902 psychrometric chart made **C6**'s relative humidity an engineering quantity, and the formal field crystallized 1910–1930. Pedagogically, again the Ganjir "conceptual engineering" one-pager — hand-drawn synopsis in the *Marks' Handbook* lineage, a genre that dignifies the cheat sheet as transmitted craft.
